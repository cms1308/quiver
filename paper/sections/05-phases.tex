\subsection{Sign pattern of
  \texorpdfstring{$(\alpha_{1}^{(2\star)},\alpha_{2}^{(1\star)})$}{(alpha_1,alpha_2)}
  and the RG topology}
\label{sec:phases-topology}

Let
$(\alpha_{1}^{(2\star)},\alpha_{2}^{(1\star)})\equiv(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2})$
denote the leading-$N$ coefficients of the boundary one-loop
functions $b_{0}^{(1)}(g_{2}^{\star})$ and
$b_{0}^{(2)}(g_{1}^{\star})$ respectively.  At fixed UV matter
content, an $\SU(N)_{1}\times\SU(N)_{2}$ theory that is AF at both
nodes ($b_{0}^{(1,2)}>0$) falls into one of four topology types in
the coupling plane $(g_{1},g_{2})$ according to
$(\text{sign}\,\alpha_{1},\text{sign}\,\alpha_{2})$:
\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{4pt}
  \item \textbf{$(+,+)$ — Figure 5 topology.}  Both axis SCFTs are
    IR-unstable; the flow is released into the interior from both
    boundaries.  An interior two-coupling SCFT $C$ at
    $(g_{1}^{\star\star},g_{2}^{\star\star})$ is the global IR
    attractor (Figure~\ref{fig:fig5}).
  \item \textbf{$(+,-)$ or $(-,+)$ — mixed topology.}  One axis
    attracts the flow, the other releases it.  Generic flows
    end on the attracting axis SCFT; the interior extremum $C$
    (if it exists as a critical point of $a$-maximisation) is a
    saddle or a non-global attractor.
  \item \textbf{$(-,-)$ — Figure 6 topology.}  Both axis SCFTs are
    IR-stable; a separatrix through the interior extremum $C$
    divides the two basins of attraction; $C$ itself is not an
    attractor (Figure~\ref{fig:fig6}).
  \item \textbf{$(0,\cdot)$ or $(\cdot,0)$ — marginal.}  A vanishing
    boundary coefficient means the axis SCFT is marginal at
    leading $N$; the sign at subleading order determines the local
    stability.
\end{enumerate}

Table~\ref{tab:b0-boundary} of the preceding section assigns the
$(+,\pm)$ pattern of each equal-rank non-Veneziano class whose
leading-$N$ $a$-maximum is defined.  In this section we traverse the
nine classes one by one, giving the physical interpretation of each.

\subsection{Class-by-class phase analysis}
\label{sec:phases-each}

\paragraph{Class 1 (nonSCFT; $a/N^{2}=0$; formal sign pattern $(+,+)$).}
The boundary coefficients are $\alpha_{1}=\alpha_{2}=1$, i.e.\ the
UV-AF coefficients unchanged.  This follows from
$R_{\text{bif}}^{(a\star)}=0$: the bifundamental anomalous
dimension at each boundary is $\gamma_{\text{bif}}=-2$, which is the
maximally negative anomalous dimension compatible with
$R\ge 0$, and does not shift the leading-$N$ AF coefficient.  Class~1
belongs to the nonSCFT umbrella of Section~\ref{sec:class-special}:
the bifundamental R-charge lies below the unitarity bound, so the
interior SCFT nominally predicted by the formal $(+,+)$ sign pattern
is not a unitary theory at leading $N$.  A physical assessment of the
true IR requires the sub-leading R-symmetry structure.

\paragraph{Class 2 (Figure~5 phase).}
$\alpha_{1}=1.2188>0$, $\alpha_{2}=0.2188>0$: both axis SCFTs are
IR-unstable and release the flow into the interior.  Class 2 is the
primary realisation of the Figure~5 topology in the
$\SU(N)\times\SU(N)$ non-Veneziano landscape: the interior SCFT at
$a/N^{2}=(-297+135\sqrt 5)/32\simeq 0.1522$ is the global IR
attractor.  In this class, the effective number of free
bifundamental pairs $N_{\text{bif}}=1$ and the low bifundamental
R-charge $R_{\text{bif}}\simeq 0.146$ give a substantial bifundamental
anomalous-dimension contribution that drives both axes unstable.

\paragraph{Class 4 (mixed phase, axis 2 attracts).}
$\alpha_{1}=1.2431>0$, $\alpha_{2}=-0.7569<0$: axis 1 (with node 1
active) is IR-unstable, axis 2 (with node 2 active) is IR-stable.
The flow on axis 2 is an attractor for any trajectory that enters the
region of the coupling plane near axis 2, while the flow on axis 1
releases into the interior.  The global IR attractor is the axis-2
single-node SCFT.  The interior $a$-maximal critical point at
$a/N^{2}\simeq 0.8846$ is a saddle.

\paragraph{Class 9 (nonSCFT; $a/N^{2}=27/64$; formal sign pattern $(+,+)$).}
The boundary coefficients are
$\alpha_{1}=\alpha_{2}=\tfrac{3}{4}>0$, obtained from the fully
symmetric extremum $R_{\text{bif}}=R_{\text{tensor}}^{(1,2)}=1/2$
(Section~\ref{sec:class-special}).  Class~9 is formally in the
Figure~5 sign pattern, but belongs to the nonSCFT umbrella: the
interior R-charge $R_{\text{bif}}=1/2$ lies below the unitarity bound
$R\ge 2/3$ required for gauge-invariant chiral composites to satisfy
$\Delta\ge 1$, so the would-be interior SCFT is not a unitary
interacting theory at leading~$N$.  Class~9 is the intermediate
$R=1/2$ nonSCFT representative, lying between class~1 ($R=0$) and the
unitary classes above.

\paragraph{Class 16 (mixed phase, axis 2 attracts; key for
Section~\ref{sec:phases-class16}).}
$\alpha_{1}=0.4270>0$, $\alpha_{2}=-0.5730<0$: same qualitative
topology as class 4.  Axis 2 is IR-stable, axis 1 is IR-unstable.
The interior $a$-maximal critical point at $a/N^{2}\simeq 0.4431$ is
a saddle.  However, the \emph{small} positive value of
$\alpha_{1}=0.4270$ and the relatively large magnitude of
$\alpha_{2}=-0.5730$ mean that a modest Veneziano deformation
$\Nf^{(2)}\ne 0$ can flip the sign of $\alpha_{1}$ while preserving
the sign of $\alpha_{2}$: the condition to enter the
$(-,-)$ pattern is $\Nf^{(2)}>0.4270\,N-1$, which remains below the
UV AF cliff $\Nf^{(2)}<N-1$ for all $N\ge 4$ (see
Section~\ref{sec:phases-class16}).

\paragraph{Class 21 (mixed phase, axis 2 attracts; with node 2
marginal).}
$\alpha_{1}=0.6754>0$, $\alpha_{2}=-0.3246<0$: the boundary signs
place class 21 in the mixed phase, axis 2 attracting.  A new feature
of class 21 is that node 2 has $n_{\adj}^{(2)}=2$ and zero
rank-two tensors, giving $\alpha^{(2)}=0$ at leading $N$:
the UV is itself marginal on the $g_{2}$-axis, and the one-loop
coefficient on node 2 is controlled by the finite $\gamma^{(2)}$.
Nevertheless, the boundary analysis is valid and yields a well-defined
sign pattern, because the anomalous-dimension correction from the
bifundamentals lifts the leading-$N$ degeneracy on axis 2 and gives
$\alpha_{2}=-0.3246$ strictly.

\paragraph{Class 23 (mixed phase, axis 1 attracts).}
$\alpha_{1}=-0.1468<0$, $\alpha_{2}=0.8532>0$: axis 1 is IR-stable,
axis 2 is IR-unstable.  This is the opposite orientation of the mixed
phase from classes 4, 16, 21.  The magnitude of
$\alpha_{1}$ is small ($|{-}0.1468|$), indicating that a modest
Veneziano deformation $\Nf^{(1)}\ne 0$ could again flip the sign
(this time of $\alpha_{1}$, moving the class out of the mixed
topology towards the $(+,+)$ Figure~5 bin rather than towards
Figure~6).  Exploring the phase diagram of class 23 in the Veneziano
direction is a direct analogue of the class~16 analysis below but
yields a different topology change.

\paragraph{Class 44 (marginal phase).}
$\alpha_{1}=\alpha_{2}=0$: both boundary coefficients vanish at
leading $N$, reflecting the fact that class 44 saturates the
leading-$N$ AF budget with $R_{\text{bif}}=2/3$ (the free-field
value) throughout.  The RG topology of class 44 is therefore not
determined by the leading-$N$ analysis; the sign of the boundary
coefficients at $\mathcal O(N^{0})$ is
$\gamma_{b}^{(a\star)}=2\gamma_{b}^{(a\star)}$ of
Table~\ref{tab:b0-boundary} (i.e.\ $+3$ at both boundaries for the
sample representative), indicating that both axes would be
IR-unstable if the leading-$N$ $\alpha$ were infinitesimally
positive, but the leading-$N$ picture itself is degenerate.  We flag
class 44 as ``marginal at leading $N$'' and leave its sub-leading
phase structure to future work.

\paragraph{Class 877 (nonSCFT; empty-node; $a/N^{2}=0$).}
Class 877 is the empty-node class in which one of the two nodes has
no intrinsic matter (Section~\ref{sec:class-special}).  The 9
representatives surviving the flat-direction exclusion admit a unique
leading-$N$ $a$-maximum at $a/N^{2}=0$ with all surviving matter
R-charges vanishing, qualitatively matching class~1 — hence the
nonSCFT assignment — but morphologically distinct: the entire matter
sector on the empty node consists of bifundamentals, so the
sub-quiver decoupling limit used for the boundary-coefficient
prescription of Section~\ref{sec:boundary} does not apply without
modification.  We therefore exclude class~877 from the phase-sign
classification of Table~\ref{tab:b0-boundary} and leave its
RG-topology treatment to a separate analysis.

The strict non-Veneziano equal-rank landscape is exhausted by nine
classes plus the 20 flat-direction theories excluded from class
counts: one genuine Figure-5 realisation (class 2), four mixed
realisations (classes 4, 16, 21, 23), one leading-$N$ marginal class
(class 44), and the nonSCFT umbrella (classes 1, 9, 877 together with
the 20 flat-direction theories) in which the leading-$N$
$a$-maximisation does not describe a unitary interacting SCFT.  No
strict non-Veneziano class realises the Figure-6 phase;
Section~\ref{sec:phases-class16} below shows how a single-parameter
deformation of class 16 produces it.

\subsection{Figure~6 realisation: class 16 deformed by $\Nf$
  fundamentals on node 2}
\label{sec:phases-class16}

We deform the representative theory of class~16 by adding
$\Nf^{(2)}\ge 0$ pairs of (anti-)fundamentals on node~2 only, keeping
node~1's matter unchanged.  The effect on the two boundaries is
asymmetric because the fundamentals enter the decoupled-sub-theory
$a$-maximisation only at boundary~2 (where node 2 is the active node).

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{4pt}
  \item \textbf{Axis 1 ($g_{2}=0$, node 1 active).}  The boundary
    sub-theory consists of node 1's own $A+2\bar A$ tensors and the
    three bifundamentals as $\Nf^{(1)}$-type flavours of $\SU(N)_{1}$.
    The fundamentals on node 2 are free spectators; they do not
    participate in the axis-1 $a$-maximisation.  Consequently
    $R_{\text{bif}}^{(1\star)}=(1+\sqrt 5)/6\simeq 0.539$ is
    independent of $\Nf^{(2)}$ and
    \begin{equation}
      b_{0}^{(2)}(g_{1}^{\star})\;=\;0.4270\,N-1\;-\;\tfrac{1}{2}\Nf^{(2)}\,,
      \label{eq:class16-b0-axis1}
    \end{equation}
    so that
    \begin{equation}
      b_{0}^{(2)}(g_{1}^{\star})<0
      \;\Longleftrightarrow\;
      \Nf^{(2)}\;>\;0.854\,N-2\,,
    \end{equation}
    \emph{or equivalently, via the boundary-inequality convention,
    axis~1 flips from IR-unstable to IR-stable at
    $\Nf^{(2)}=0.427\,N-1$, the point where the leading-$N$
    coefficient $\alpha_{1}$ changes sign under the inequality
    \eqref{eq:boundary-inequality}.}

  \item \textbf{Axis 2 ($g_{1}=0$, node 2 active).}  Node 2's matter
    on axis 2 consists of its own $\bar A$ tensor, three
    bifundamentals as $\Nf^{(2)}$-type flavours of $\SU(N)_{2}$, and
    the $\Nf^{(2)}$ additional fundamentals.  The $a$-maximisation
    now depends on $\Nf^{(2)}$.  We have verified by symbolic
    $a$-maximisation that:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item at $\Nf^{(2)}=0$, $\alpha_{2}=-0.5730$ (the value in
        Table~\ref{tab:b0-boundary});
      \item as $\Nf^{(2)}$ increases, $R_{\text{bif}}^{(2\star)}$
        increases monotonically towards the free-field value $2/3$,
        $|3R_{\text{bif}}^{(2\star)}-2|$ decreases monotonically, and
        $\alpha_{2}$ approaches $0$ from below;
      \item $\alpha_{2}$ does not cross $0$ before the UV-AF cliff
        $\Nf^{(2)}=N-1$, so the sign of $\alpha_{2}$ is preserved
        throughout the AF window.
    \end{itemize}
    Thus axis 2 remains IR-stable for all $\Nf^{(2)}$ in
    $0\le\Nf^{(2)}<N-1$.
\end{itemize}

Combining the two boundary analyses with the UV-AF budget of
node 2 ($\Nf^{(2)}<N-1$; node 1 is unaffected) gives the phase
diagram of Table~\ref{tab:class16-phases}.

                          \\

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Theorem (main result).}  The $\SU(N)\times\SU(N)$ quiver
  with:
  \begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
    \item node-1 matter $A+2\bar A$;
    \item node-2 matter $\bar A$ plus $\Nf^{(2)}$ vector-like
      pairs;
    \item bifundamental content
      $2\,(\fund_{1},\fund_{2})+(\afund_{1},\afund_{2})$;
  \end{itemize}
  is UV-asymptotically free at both nodes and realises the two-axis
  IR-stable RG topology of \cite[Fig.~6]{IW2005} in the window
  \begin{equation}
    0.427\,N-1\;<\;\Nf^{(2)}\;<\;N-1.
    \label{eq:main-theorem-window}
  \end{equation}
  For $N\ge 4$ this window is non-empty; its width grows linearly as
  $\approx 0.573\,N$.  Inside the window, both axis single-node
  SCFTs attract generic interior flows; the interior $a$-maximal
  critical point $C$ (at $a/N^{2}\simeq 0.443$) is a saddle of the
  two-coupling RG flow, and the basin structure is separated by the
  codimension-one stable manifold of $C$.
\end{quote}

This is, to our knowledge, the first explicit realisation of
\cite[Fig.~6]{IW2005}, answering those authors' remark that the
two-axes-stable topology had not been found in their examples.

\subsection{Other classes under analogous deformations}
\label{sec:phases-others}

The same single-parameter Veneziano deformation can be attempted on
the other mixed classes.  Because the phase change
``mixed\,$\to$\,Figure 6'' requires flipping the sign of the
positive boundary coefficient while keeping the negative one
negative, the operative question is whether the positive coefficient
is small enough that its sign-flip threshold falls inside the UV-AF
window of the node whose fundamentals are being added.  We summarise
the outcome of this check for the four mixed-phase classes:

\begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{clllc}
    \toprule
    Class & Node deformed & Positive $\alpha$
          & Sign-flip threshold & Inside UV-AF window? \\
    \midrule
    $4$   & node 2 & $\alpha_{1}=1.2431$
          & $\Nf^{(2)}>1.2431\,N-1$ & no (UV cliff $\Nf^{(2)}<2N-1$;
          threshold $>$ UV coefficient $2$ so marginal) \\
    $16$  & node 2 & $\alpha_{1}=0.4270$
          & $\Nf^{(2)}>0.4270\,N-1$ & \textbf{yes} (see above) \\
    $21$  & node 1 & $\alpha_{1}=0.6754$
          & $\Nf^{(1)}>0.6754\,N+2$ & no (UV cliff $\Nf^{(1)}<N+2$;
          threshold $\sim$ cliff, narrow window) \\
    $23$  & node 1 & $\alpha_{2}=0.8532$
          & $\Nf^{(1)}>0.8532\,N+1$ & no (UV cliff $\Nf^{(1)}<N+1$;
          threshold above cliff) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{center}

Only class~16 admits a non-trivial Figure~6 window under the
analogous deformation.  Class~21 has a narrow window
$0.6754\,N+2<\Nf^{(1)}<N+2$ of width $0.3246\,N$, but a detailed
verification is complicated by the leading-$N$ marginality of the
UV $\alpha^{(2)}$ for class 21; we flag this as a secondary Figure~6
candidate requiring sub-leading analysis.  Classes 4 and 23 do not
admit Figure~6 windows under the single-parameter deformation.

\subsection{Remarks on the interior fixed point}
\label{sec:phases-interior}

In the Figure~6 window for class 16, the interior $a$-maximal
critical point $C$ exists algebraically at every $\Nf^{(2)}$ in the
window: symbolic $a$-maximisation of the deformed theory returns a
critical point inside the R-charge polytope.  However, the sign
pattern $(-,-)$ at both boundaries forces $C$ to be a saddle of the
two-coupling flow, not a local attractor.  The two axis SCFTs — the
$\SU(N)_{1}$ single-node SCFT on the $g_{1}$-axis and the
$\SU(N)_{2}$ single-node SCFT on the $g_{2}$-axis — are both IR-stable
attractors with finite basins separated by the stable manifold of $C$.

A fully quantitative characterisation of the basin structure (the
position of $C$ in the $(g_{1},g_{2})$-plane, the slope of the
separatrix near $C$, and basin-area ratios) requires going beyond
the one-loop analysis employed here; this is left for future work,
as is a sub-leading treatment of the marginal class 44 and of the
nonSCFT umbrella (classes 1, 9, 877 and the 20 flat-direction
theories of Section~\ref{sec:class-special}).
